## Database Recommendation

For a healthcare startup building a patient management system, I would recommend **MySQL** as the primary database for the core application. Patient records, appointments, prescriptions, billing details, and lab updates are transactional data that must remain accurate and consistent at all times. This fits well with **ACID** guarantees: atomicity, consistency, isolation, and durability. In healthcare, even a small inconsistency can create serious operational or patient-safety problems. For example, if a prescription update succeeds but the linked patient record update fails, the system may contain conflicting information. A relational database is better suited to enforcing constraints, preserving data integrity, and supporting structured relationships between entities such as patients, doctors, visits, and treatments.

From the perspective of the **CAP theorem**, healthcare systems usually prioritize **consistency** very strongly for operational workflows. MongoDB offers flexibility and high scalability, but NoSQL systems are more often associated with **BASE-style** trade-offs, where eventual consistency can be acceptable for some use cases. That flexibility is useful, but it is not the most important requirement for the main patient management system, where correctness and auditability matter more than schema flexibility.

My answer would change somewhat if the company also needed a **fraud detection module**. Fraud detection often involves processing large volumes of semi-structured or fast-changing data such as device logs, payment events, usage patterns, and anomaly signals. That kind of workload can benefit from MongoDB because it handles flexible schemas and scale-out storage well. In that case, I would recommend a **hybrid architecture**: MySQL for the core patient management transactions, and MongoDB for fraud-related analytics or signal ingestion. This approach keeps the transactional system reliable while giving the fraud module a more scalable and adaptable data platform.
