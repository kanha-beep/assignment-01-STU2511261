## Storage Systems

I would use multiple storage systems because the hospital has four distinct workloads. For readmission prediction, the historical treatment and encounter data should flow from the hospital's transactional EHR system into a curated analytical store such as a cloud data warehouse or lakehouse. That gives the ML team clean longitudinal data for feature engineering and model training without disturbing operational systems. For doctors asking questions in plain English, I would keep authoritative patient records in the OLTP system, then create document chunks from clinical notes, discharge summaries, and prior diagnoses and store their embeddings in a vector database. That supports semantic retrieval while keeping the source of truth in the core clinical database. For monthly management reporting, I would use the same warehouse or lakehouse because occupancy, departmental cost, and utilization reporting are classic OLAP workloads. For real-time ICU vitals, I would use a time-series or streaming-oriented store that can ingest high-frequency telemetry efficiently, then archive older data into the analytical platform for long-term analysis.

## OLTP vs OLAP Boundary

The OLTP boundary ends at systems that capture day-to-day hospital operations: patient registration, admissions, medication updates, lab results, billing events, and clinician note entry. These systems need strict consistency, low-latency writes, and auditability. The OLAP boundary begins once operational data is replicated into analytical pipelines for aggregation, modeling, and search enrichment. At that point, data is transformed into star-schema facts, feature tables, reporting marts, and vectorized text chunks. This separation is important because doctors and nurses should not experience slower transactional performance just because analysts are running monthly cost reports or data scientists are retraining a readmission model. It also keeps governance clearer, since regulated clinical updates remain in controlled operational systems while reporting and AI workloads use curated downstream copies.

## Trade-offs

One significant trade-off is architectural complexity. Using a relational OLTP database, a warehouse or lakehouse, a vector database, and a streaming vitals store is more complex than forcing everything into one platform. It increases integration work, monitoring needs, and governance overhead. There is also a risk of freshness gaps, especially when doctors expect recent events to appear quickly in search or reporting layers. I would mitigate that by defining clear data contracts between systems, using automated pipelines with validation checks, and enforcing strong metadata management so every team knows which store is authoritative for which purpose. For the most time-sensitive paths, such as ICU monitoring and recent patient-history retrieval, I would use near-real-time ingestion and explicit latency targets for synchronization. That keeps the design maintainable while still matching the technical needs of each workload.
